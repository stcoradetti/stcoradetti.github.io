A rememberance of Presto

We recenetly lost our beloved Presto, snuggle feind and hiker extraorinaire. To chase the blues away I thought I'd gather some of my favorite pictures of him on adventures with us and make some notes for posterity. It turns out there were a lot of them. Enjoy.


June 2014 - Bolinas Ridge Trai in Marin County, CA
Lori and I had just started dating. She was pretty new to California and I didn't have much experience hiking with dogs out there. I was pretty taken aback to find out the marquee state and national parks banned all dogs on trails. It seemed at first we be limited to pretty lame trails like this one, a long slow ramp up a dead-grass ridge under the baking sun. We would find though that there were quite a lot of awesome places we could still go. Always taking Presto meant we ended up going a ton of less well known places where we enjoyed some spectacular scenery, usually with zero crowds whatsoever.

July 2014 - The Lost Coast, north of Fort Bragg, CA
Our first backpacking adventure with Presto was on 'The Lost Coast' of Northern Califorina, a remote 30-mile stretch where 4,000 foot mountains drop right to the beach and you have to time your hike to the tide table. It's 'Lost' becuase it was too rugged to build a road there. Seems like 'Saved Coast' would be better name for it. Jim Craig and Wilfried Jonkers came along and we had a grand fourth of July weekend isolated from the entire world except a few parties of extremely adventurous surfers.

We tried getting Presto to carry some of his own gear on this trip, but he really did not enjoy it. He would get free sherpa services from here on out. Keeping the booties refreshed was pretty critical on the rough rocky sand on this stretch. A few sections had awkward rocks he need to be carried over, but mostly he did fine.

July 2014 - Levitt Peak (11569 ft), near Sonora Pass, CA
We knew that lots of national forest land was open for dogs, so soon after we started to dispair of our options around the SF Bay area, we went straight for the Sierra. This was the highest peak I'd summited by over a thousand feet and I barely made the summit. Presto seemed unbothered. We know he was working hard though because his heartbeat was very regular. Usually whippet hearts just have beat slowly and randomly unless they're running. It's like their oxygen delivery is so high they don't even have to regulate it when they're not running around.

August 2014 - Jofree Lakes (5000 ft), north of Whistler, BC
Lori went up to British Columbia for the North American Orienteering Champs I came along and did some orienteering too, but mostly hung out with Presto and got her to take some side hiking trips. At the Jofree Lakes we found this 200 foot perfectly strait pine trunk jutting way out into the neon blue glacial lake and we just had to jump in even though it was literally just above freezing. Presto was so nervous to be left alone on the shore he came out on the log to shiver next to Lori.

August 2014 - Pacific Crest Trail (6500 ft), just west of Rainier National Park, WA
On the way home we stopped by Seattle to see a friend of Lori's and hit up the PCT just outside of Ranier National Park and found a pretty impressive minor peak near Sourdough Gap.

Close up on the peak

November 2014 - Mt Tallac (9739 ft), near Lake Tahoe, CA
I was deep in Ph.D. thesis hell and this hike was just what I needed. We brought along Carol and Greg Walker of Lori's ever expansive orienteering circle.

February 2015 - Heart Lake (6500 ft), near Mt Shasta, CA
Our first attempt to explore the Mt Shasta area. We had some mildly succesfully backcountry skiing on this trip on the side of Mt. Shasta but just sort of puttered around a bit. This was the trip when Presto discovered that the freeze-thaw cycle makes California snow so dense he can just run around on top of it, even whent it's six feet deep. He was thrilled. Here we are the next day at a minor peak overlooking Heart Lake, near yet another section of the Pacific Crest trail. We were about to start our hike at a different spot and try to get up here with a long 4,000 foot slog, but at the trailhead a couple with a whole pile of Huskies saw our snow shoes and redirected us to a higher, more interesting trailhead. This was the genesis of one of hiking commandments of 'always listen to reccommendations given at trailheads'.
